A sigil (from Latin sigillum, meaning "seal") is a symbolic mark, sign, or image believed to carry magical or representational power. The word has two main contexts:
In magic/occultism: A sigil is a symbol created with specific intent — a practitioner would design or derive a unique glyph meant to manifest a desire or represent a spiritual entity. This usage was common in medieval grimoires and is still used in modern chaos magic.
In heraldry/symbolism: A sigil is essentially a personal seal or badge — a unique emblem identifying an individual, family, or institution. This is the sense closest to how Game of Thrones popularized the term.

Medieval noble houses — what did they actually use?
The terminology varied by context:
TermWhat it meantArms / Coat of ArmsThe full heraldic design on a shield — the primary identifier of a noble houseBannerA large square or rectangular cloth bearing the full coat of arms, flown from castles or carried into battle by a knight of high rankStandardA long tapered flag with the house's colors, badge, and motto — used to rally troops in the fieldPennon / PennantA smaller personal flag flown from a knight's lanceBadgeA simpler symbol (not the full arms) used on livery, retainers' clothing, etc. — e.g., the Lancastrian red roseSigilUsed more in the context of seals on wax — personal marks pressed into documentsDeviceA personal emblem, sometimes with a motto, used decoratively
So a medieval lord would have arms (the shield design), a banner (the flag version of those arms), and a badge (a simpler emblem for everyday use on retainers and property). The word sigil was more associated with wax seals and written documents than with flags.
The Game of Thrones usage — "House Stark's sigil is the direwolf" — is a modern popularization that blends the heraldic and magical senses into one convenient word. Historically, a herald would have said arms or device, not sigil.
